Flail Chest

Flail chest is a life-threatening blunt chest trauma pattern where multiple adjacent ribs are fractured in multiple places, creating a free-floating chest wall segment. This module focuses on RT recognition, paradoxical movement, hypoventilation, pulmonary contusion risk, oxygenation/ventilation support, pain control coordination, and escalation when respiratory failure develops.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this flail chest module, learners should be able to connect blunt trauma mechanics, paradoxical chest wall movement, pain-limited ventilation, pulmonary contusion, diagnostic findings, RT assessment priorities, and escalation decisions.

1. Explain the mechanism
Describe how multiple rib fractures create an unstable chest wall segment and impair ventilation.
2. Recognize key findings
Identify paradoxical movement, severe pain, tachypnea, hypoxemia, splinting, and contusion-related deterioration.
3. Interpret trauma data
Use chest imaging, ABGs, SpO₂ trends, work of breathing, and auscultation to judge severity.
4. Escalate appropriately
Prioritize oxygenation, ventilation, pain control coordination, positive pressure support, and urgent escalation for fatigue or shock.

Disease Snapshot

Flail chest occurs when a segment of the thoracic cage loses structural continuity from the rest of the chest wall. The unstable segment may move inward during inspiration and outward during expiration, reducing effective ventilation and signaling significant chest trauma.

What it is:
Two or more adjacent ribs fractured in two or more places, creating a free-floating segment.
Primary problem:
Mechanical instability plus pain-limited breathing and frequent underlying lung injury.
Classic sign:
Paradoxical chest wall movement.
High-yield complication:
Pulmonary contusion with worsening hypoxemia over time.
Acute danger:
Hypoventilation, respiratory fatigue, pneumothorax, hemothorax, shock, or respiratory failure.
RT priority:
Assess oxygenation, ventilation, WOB, pain-limited breathing, breath sounds, and need for positive pressure support.

RT Clinical Connection

For RTs, flail chest is not just a rib fracture problem. The immediate question is whether the patient can ventilate deeply enough despite pain, chest wall instability, and possible lung contusion.

Board Prep Frame

Associate flail chest with blunt trauma, paradoxical motion, severe pain, pulmonary contusion, hypoxemia, possible pneumothorax/hemothorax, and escalation to positive pressure ventilation when respiratory failure or fatigue develops.

Anatomy & Pathophysiology

Flail chest begins with high-energy blunt trauma. Rib fractures separate a segment of the chest wall from the stable rib cage. Pain causes splinting and shallow breathing, while the unstable segment decreases chest wall efficiency. The underlying lung is often bruised, producing pulmonary contusion and impaired gas exchange.

Blunt trauma → multiple rib fractures → unstable chest wall segment → paradoxical motion + severe pain → shallow breathing and poor cough → atelectasis/retained secretions + pulmonary contusion → hypoxemia and respiratory failure risk.

Clinical Manifestations

Flail chest presentation depends on injury severity, pain, lung contusion, and associated trauma. RT assessment should focus on work of breathing, chest wall movement, breath sounds, SpO₂, mental status, cough strength, and gas exchange.

Finding TypeWhat the RT May See
Trauma patternRecent MVC, fall, crush injury, or direct chest blow.
Chest movementParadoxical inward movement during inspiration and outward movement during expiration.
Pain behaviorSplinting, shallow breathing, reluctance to cough, guarding.
Breath soundsDiminished sounds, crackles from contusion, or absent sounds if pneumothorax/hemothorax occurs.
Oxygenation cluesLow SpO₂, increasing FiO₂ requirement, cyanosis in severe cases.
Ventilation cluesTachypnea early; rising PaCO₂, fatigue, or altered mental status late.

Severity & Red Flags

The danger in flail chest is not the visible deformity alone. The danger is progressive failure to oxygenate or ventilate due to lung contusion, pain, fatigue, and associated thoracic injury.

Red FlagWhy It Matters
Increasing oxygen requirementMay indicate worsening contusion, atelectasis, pneumothorax, or hemothorax.
Rising PaCO₂ or falling pHSuggests ventilatory failure from fatigue or inadequate ventilation.
Altered mental status or exhaustionMay indicate hypoxemia, hypercapnia, shock, or severe fatigue.
Absent unilateral breath soundsConsider pneumothorax or hemothorax.
Hypotension, JVD, tracheal shiftPossible tension pneumothorax or shock; requires urgent escalation.
Poor cough with retained secretionsRaises atelectasis and pneumonia risk.

Management & Treatment

Management focuses on oxygenation, ventilation, pain control, pulmonary hygiene, and treatment of associated injuries. Stabilizing the chest wall manually is not the primary modern solution; restoring effective ventilation and addressing complications are the priority.

Core Management Priorities

PriorityExamplesWhy It Helps
OxygenationSupplemental O₂, HFNC, NIV, or invasive ventilation as indicated.Corrects hypoxemia from contusion, atelectasis, or hypoventilation.
Pain control coordinationAnalgesia, regional blocks/epidural per provider plan.Allows deeper breathing, cough, and mobility.
Ventilatory supportNIV or intubation/mechanical ventilation when failing.Reduces WOB and supports gas exchange.
Pulmonary hygieneIncentive spirometry, coughing, splinting, secretion clearance when stable.Prevents atelectasis and secretion retention.
Treat complicationsChest tube for pneumothorax/hemothorax if ordered.Restores lung expansion and reduces life threats.
Surgical stabilizationRib fixation in selected severe cases.May improve mechanics and reduce ventilator duration in selected patients.

Ventilation Strategy & Escalation

Positive pressure can stabilize the chest wall internally and support ventilation, but the RT must monitor for associated trauma risks such as pneumothorax, worsening compliance from contusion, and shock.

Watch for:
Increasing WOB, falling SpO₂, rising PaCO₂, altered mental status, shock, or worsening chest mechanics.
Support concept:
Use positive pressure when needed to improve oxygenation, ventilation, and chest wall mechanics.
Monitoring:
Trend airway pressures, SpO₂, ABGs, breath sounds, chest tube status, and hemodynamics.

Board Pearl

For board-style questions, choose oxygenation and ventilatory support based on clinical failure signs. Do not delay escalation in a flail chest patient who is tiring, hypoxemic despite oxygen, or developing hypercapnia/acidosis.

Respiratory Therapist Role

The RT is central to recognizing deterioration, delivering oxygen/ventilatory support, encouraging lung expansion when safe, monitoring ABG trends, and communicating changes quickly to the trauma team.

FAQ

Common Flail Chest Questions

What is the simplest way to understand flail chest?

Flail chest is a chest wall instability problem caused by multiple rib fractures that create a free-floating segment.

Why does the segment move paradoxically?

Because it is no longer connected normally to the stable rib cage. Negative pressure during inspiration can pull the segment inward instead of outward.

Is paradoxical movement the only problem?

No. Pain-limited ventilation and pulmonary contusion often drive hypoxemia and respiratory failure risk.

Why is pulmonary contusion important?

Bruised lung tissue can develop edema and bleeding, decreasing compliance and worsening V/Q mismatch over time.

When should ventilation be escalated?

Escalate when the patient has persistent hypoxemia, rising PaCO₂, acidosis, fatigue, altered mental status, or cannot maintain adequate ventilation.

Why is pain control part of respiratory care?

Without adequate pain control, the patient splints, breathes shallowly, coughs poorly, and develops atelectasis or retained secretions.

What complication should be suspected with sudden unilateral absent breath sounds?

After chest trauma, sudden unilateral absent breath sounds should raise concern for pneumothorax or hemothorax, especially if distress or hypotension worsens.

Summary & Clinical Pearls

Flail chest is a severe blunt chest trauma pattern that creates an unstable chest wall segment. The RT must connect paradoxical movement, severe pain, pulmonary contusion, hypoxemia, secretion retention, and fatigue into one clinical picture.

Must know:
Flail chest involves multiple adjacent ribs fractured in multiple places.
Board pearl:
Pulmonary contusion and pain-limited ventilation often drive gas exchange problems.
RT pearl:
Assess WOB, chest movement, breath sounds, SpO₂, ABGs, cough, pain, and fatigue trends.
Common mistake:
Do not focus only on the ribs. Watch for pneumothorax, hemothorax, contusion, and ventilatory failure.
