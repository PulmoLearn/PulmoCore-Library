Disease Snapshot

Pneumonia is an infection and inflammatory process within the alveoli and surrounding lung tissue. The primary RT concern is impaired gas exchange caused by alveolar filling, secretion burden, reduced ventilation to affected regions, and increased work of breathing.

What it is:
Infection of the lung parenchyma with inflammatory alveolar filling.
Primary problem:
Consolidation causes poor ventilation to perfused lung units.
Classic symptoms:
Fever, cough, sputum, pleuritic pain, dyspnea, tachypnea, crackles.
Key diagnostic clue:
Clinical infection pattern plus new infiltrate or consolidation.
Acute danger:
Refractory hypoxemia, sepsis, fatigue, rising PaCO₂, altered mental status.
RT priority:
Assess oxygenation, ventilation, WOB, secretion clearance, and escalation needs.

RT Clinical Connection

For RTs, pneumonia is not just an antibiotic problem. It is a gas-exchange and airway-clearance problem. The RT must determine whether the patient is oxygenating, ventilating, clearing secretions, protecting the airway, and responding to support.

Board Prep Frame

Immediately associate pneumonia with fever, cough, sputum, crackles, infiltrate/consolidation, V/Q mismatch, hypoxemia, secretion management, and escalation when oxygen needs rise or PaCO₂ increases.

Anatomy & Pathophysiology

Pneumonia causes inflammation in the distal airways and alveoli. Fluid, cells, organisms, and debris fill airspaces, reducing ventilation to affected lung units while perfusion may continue. This produces V/Q mismatch and shunt-like physiology.

Pathogen reaches lower airway → immune response → alveolar exudate/consolidation → impaired ventilation → V/Q mismatch and shunt-like hypoxemia → increased WOB → possible respiratory failure, sepsis, or ARDS.

What Changes During Pneumonia

Pneumonia ChangeWhat HappensWhy It Matters
Alveolar fillingExudate occupies airspaces that should contain gas.Reduces ventilation and creates hypoxemia.
ConsolidationLung tissue becomes dense and poorly aerated.Produces crackles, bronchial breath sounds, and infiltrate.
V/Q mismatchPerfusion continues through poorly ventilated areas.Creates hypoxemia that may worsen quickly.
Secretion burdenSputum and mucus may be difficult to clear.Can worsen atelectasis, obstruction, and work of breathing.

ABG Progression Concept

Early pneumonia may show hypoxemia with respiratory alkalosis from tachypnea. If the patient tires, PaCO₂ may rise and pH may fall. Rising PaCO₂ in a pneumonia patient with increased work of breathing is a red flag for ventilatory failure.

Danger Sign

A patient with pneumonia who becomes drowsy, hypotensive, cyanotic, or unable to maintain oxygenation despite increasing support may be progressing toward respiratory failure, sepsis, or ARDS.

Clinical Manifestations

Pneumonia presentation can range from mild cough to severe sepsis and respiratory failure. RT assessment should focus on oxygenation, ventilation, work of breathing, breath sounds, sputum, mental status, and response to support.

Finding TypeWhat the RT May See
SymptomsFever, chills, cough, sputum, dyspnea, pleuritic chest pain, fatigue.
AppearanceTachypnea, accessory muscle use, anxiety, diaphoresis, cyanosis in severe cases.
Breath soundsCrackles, bronchial breath sounds, diminished sounds, rhonchi, dullness to percussion.
Oxygenation cluesLow SpO₂, low PaO₂, increasing oxygen requirement.
Ventilation cluesEarly low PaCO₂ from tachypnea; rising PaCO₂ with fatigue or failure.
Systemic cluesFever, tachycardia, hypotension, altered mentation, poor perfusion.

Important Teaching Point

Do not assume pneumonia is stable because the patient is already on oxygen. Increasing oxygen needs, worsening mentation, rising PaCO₂, hypotension, or exhaustion should trigger escalation.

Severity & Red Flags

The key question is not simply “Does this patient have pneumonia?” The key question is whether the patient is maintaining oxygenation, ventilation, perfusion, airway protection, and secretion clearance.

Rising PaCO₂ with fatigue or altered mentationSuggests ventilatory failure.
Hypotension, mottling, poor perfusionPossible sepsis or shock.

Management & Treatment

Pneumonia management targets infection, oxygenation, ventilation, secretion clearance, hydration/nutrition support, and prevention of deterioration. RTs help determine whether the current support is enough.

Respiratory Support Options

NeedPossible SupportRT Reasoning
Mild hypoxemiaNasal cannula or simple maskMonitor SpO₂, WOB, and response.
Moderate/severe hypoxemiaVenturi, nonrebreather, or HFNC depending on protocol and severityEscalate based on oxygen requirement and WOB.
High WOB but protected airwayHFNC or NIV in selected patientsAssess contraindications and failure signs.
Ventilatory failure or airway protection failureIntubation and mechanical ventilationUrgent escalation when CO₂ rises, pH falls, or mental status worsens.
Secretion retentionCough coaching, PEP, CPT if indicated, suctioningSupport clearance without exhausting patient.

Increase oxygen support and position to optimize ventilation.
Assess secretions, cough strength, breath sounds, and need for suction/clearance.
Notify provider or rapid response for red flags or failure to respond.

Airway Clearance & Prevention

Pneumonia may increase secretions and weaken effective ventilation. RT airway-clearance decisions should be individualized based on cough strength, secretion volume, breath sounds, oxygenation, fatigue, and contraindications.

Mobilize secretions:
Cough coaching, hydration as ordered, early mobility, and PEP therapy when appropriate.
Suction when needed:
Use suctioning when the patient cannot clear secretions or an artificial airway is present.
Prevent VAP:
Support oral care, head-of-bed elevation, suctioning as indicated, and ventilator bundle adherence.

FAQ

Common Pneumonia Questions

Why does pneumonia cause hypoxemia?

Alveoli fill with inflammatory material, so affected lung regions are perfused but poorly ventilated. This creates low V/Q and shunt-like physiology.

Is pneumonia only an oxygen problem?

No. Pneumonia often starts with oxygenation impairment, but severe disease can cause fatigue, rising PaCO₂, acidemia, and ventilatory failure.

When should the RT worry about sepsis?

Hypotension, altered mental status, poor perfusion, fever, tachycardia, and worsening respiratory status may suggest sepsis and require urgent escalation.

What makes aspiration pneumonia different?

Aspiration pneumonia follows inhalation of oral or gastric contents, often from vomiting, dysphagia, altered mental status, sedation, or poor airway protection.

What is the RT role in sputum collection?

RTs may help obtain an expectorated specimen or suctioned sample when ordered, especially when the patient has an artificial airway or weak cough.

When is airway clearance most important?

When secretions are copious, cough is weak, breath sounds are coarse or diminished, atelectasis is suspected, or secretion retention is worsening gas exchange.

Summary & Clinical Pearls

Pneumonia is an infectious lung disease that fills alveoli with inflammatory material, causing consolidation, V/Q mismatch, shunt-like hypoxemia, secretion burden, and increased work of breathing. Severe cases may progress to sepsis, ARDS, ventilatory failure, or airway protection failure.

Must know:
Pneumonia causes alveolar filling and impaired oxygenation.
Board pearl:
Rising PaCO₂ in a tiring pneumonia patient is dangerous.
RT pearl:
Assess oxygenation, ventilation, WOB, breath sounds, sputum, cough, and response to therapy.
Common mistake:
Do not focus only on antibiotics; RTs must recognize respiratory failure and airway clearance needs.
