Respiratory Disease Module

ARDS

Acute respiratory distress syndrome is an acute, diffuse inflammatory lung injury that causes increased alveolar-capillary permeability, noncardiogenic pulmonary edema, decreased lung compliance, severe hypoxemia, and bilateral pulmonary opacities. This module focuses on RT-level recognition, oxygenation assessment, P/F ratio interpretation, lung-protective ventilation, PEEP, proning, escalation, and board-style clinical reasoning.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this ARDS module, learners should be able to connect alveolar-capillary membrane injury, permeability edema, refractory hypoxemia, decreased compliance, diagnostic criteria, ventilator strategy, and escalation priorities.

1. Explain pathophysiology
Describe how diffuse inflammatory lung injury causes permeability edema, alveolar flooding, surfactant dysfunction, atelectasis, shunt, and reduced compliance.
2. Differentiate ARDS from cardiogenic edema
Compare noncardiogenic permeability edema with hydrostatic pulmonary edema using history, imaging, echo/hemodynamics, fluid status, and response to therapy.
3. Interpret assessment data
Use SpO₂, ABGs, FiO₂, PEEP/CPAP level, P/F ratio, chest imaging, plateau pressure, compliance, and work of breathing to determine severity.
4. Escalate appropriately
Recognize red flags such as refractory hypoxemia, rising oxygen/PEEP requirement, worsening compliance, high plateau pressure, shock, altered mental status, and ventilator dyssynchrony.

Disease Snapshot

ARDS is an acute hypoxemic respiratory failure syndrome. The primary issue is not bronchospasm; it is alveolar-capillary injury that allows protein-rich fluid to flood alveoli, collapse lung units, increase shunt, and make the lungs stiff and difficult to ventilate.

What it is:
Acute inflammatory lung injury with bilateral opacities and hypoxemia.
Primary problem:
Alveolar flooding, collapse, shunt, and decreased compliance.
Classic symptoms:
Dyspnea, tachypnea, hypoxemia, crackles, increased WOB, and escalating oxygen need.
Key diagnostic clue:
Berlin pattern: acute timing, bilateral opacities, noncardiogenic edema, and low P/F ratio on PEEP/CPAP ≥5.
Acute danger:
Refractory hypoxemia, severe P/F ratio, high plateau pressure, shock, or worsening compliance.
RT priority:
Assess oxygenation, ventilation, P/F ratio, lung mechanics, PEEP response, synchrony, and escalation needs.

RT Clinical Connection

For RTs, ARDS is a lung-protection problem. The RT must balance oxygenation support with prevention of ventilator-induced lung injury by monitoring tidal volume by predicted body weight, plateau pressure, driving pressure concepts, PEEP response, and patient synchrony.

Board Prep Frame

Immediately associate ARDS with acute onset after an insult, bilateral opacities, noncardiogenic pulmonary edema, P/F ratio severity, refractory hypoxemia, low compliance, lung-protective ventilation, PEEP, and proning for severe hypoxemia.

Anatomy & Pathophysiology

ARDS begins when a direct or indirect insult triggers diffuse inflammation in the lung. The alveolar-capillary barrier becomes leaky, protein-rich fluid enters alveoli, surfactant function worsens, alveoli collapse, and gas exchange becomes dominated by shunt and V/Q mismatch.

Sepsis/pneumonia/aspiration/trauma → inflammatory lung injury → leaky alveolar-capillary membrane → protein-rich edema + surfactant dysfunction → atelectasis and shunt → refractory hypoxemia + stiff lungs.

What Changes During ARDS

ARDS ChangeWhat HappensWhy It Matters
Alveolar-capillary injuryThe membrane becomes leaky from inflammation.Protein-rich fluid enters alveoli and impairs gas exchange.
Noncardiogenic pulmonary edemaFluid accumulates from permeability, not primarily hydrostatic pressure.ARDS must be distinguished from heart failure/fluid overload.
Surfactant dysfunctionAlveoli become unstable and collapse.Compliance falls and opening pressures increase.
Intrapulmonary shuntBlood passes alveoli that are perfused but not ventilated.Hypoxemia may not correct easily with oxygen alone.
Low complianceThe lungs become stiff.Ventilator pressures rise and lung-protective ventilation becomes essential.

Oxygenation Severity Concept

ARDS severity is commonly framed by the PaO₂/FiO₂ ratio while the patient is receiving PEEP or CPAP of at least 5 cm H₂O. Mild ARDS is P/F 201–300, moderate is 101–200, and severe is 100 or less. A falling P/F ratio means worsening oxygen transfer.

Danger Sign

A patient who requires rapidly increasing FiO₂ and PEEP while the P/F ratio remains low may be progressing to severe ARDS and needs escalation planning.

Etiology & Risk Factors

ARDS usually follows a direct lung injury or an indirect systemic inflammatory insult. Common causes include sepsis, pneumonia, aspiration, trauma, pancreatitis, massive transfusion, inhalation injury, and shock.

Cause or Risk FactorWhy It MattersTeaching Focus
SepsisMost common indirect trigger; systemic inflammation injures lung capillaries.Treat source, support perfusion, and monitor oxygenation closely.
PneumoniaDirect infectious lung injury can progress to diffuse inflammation.Monitor infiltrates, cultures, oxygen needs, and secretion burden.
AspirationGastric contents chemically injure alveoli.Airway protection, suctioning, and early oxygenation assessment matter.
Trauma/ShockInflammatory and perfusion injury can trigger ARDS.Assess perfusion, lactate trend, and worsening oxygenation.
Massive transfusionTransfusion-related lung injury can cause acute hypoxemia.Trend timing, CXR, oxygenation, and hemodynamics.
PancreatitisInflammatory mediators can indirectly injure the lung.Watch for systemic inflammatory deterioration.

Clinical Presentation

ARDS presentation usually includes acute dyspnea, tachypnea, hypoxemia, bilateral pulmonary opacities, crackles or diminished aeration, and increased work of breathing. RT assessment should focus on oxygenation response, ventilatory support needs, lung mechanics, synchrony, and signs of worsening failure.

Finding TypeWhat the RT May See
SymptomsAcute dyspnea, tachypnea, hypoxemia, anxiety, and increasing oxygen requirement.
AppearanceDistress, accessory muscle use, diaphoresis, cyanosis, altered mental status, or shock.
Breath soundsCrackles, decreased aeration, or coarse/diminished breath sounds depending on edema and atelectasis.
Oxygenation cluesLow SpO₂/PaO₂ despite supplemental oxygen; falling P/F ratio.
Ventilation cluesPaCO₂ may be low from tachypnea or rise with fatigue/dead space/ventilatory failure.
Response cluesImprovement in SpO₂, PaO₂, P/F ratio, WOB, ventilator synchrony, and pressure/compliance trends.

Important Teaching Point

An ARDS patient with worsening mental status, escalating FiO₂/PEEP needs, falling blood pressure, or rising plateau pressures is not stable. These are escalation cues.

Diagnostics & Assessment

Diagnostic data helps confirm ARDS criteria, quantify oxygenation impairment, identify the precipitating cause, and rule out cardiogenic pulmonary edema or other causes of acute hypoxemia.

Berlin Criteria and Oxygenation

Criterion/DataARDS Pattern
TimingWithin 1 week of known insult or new/worsening respiratory symptoms.
ImagingBilateral opacities not fully explained by effusions, collapse, or nodules.
Origin of edemaRespiratory failure not fully explained by heart failure or fluid overload.
P/F ratioMild 201–300, moderate 101–200, severe ≤100 with PEEP/CPAP ≥5 cm H₂O.
Lung mechanicsReduced compliance and risk of elevated plateau/driving pressures.

ABG Patterns

PatternMeaning
Low PaO₂ on high FiO₂Severe oxygenation failure; calculate P/F ratio.
Low P/F ratioQuantifies ARDS severity and escalation risk.
Rising PaCO₂ with fatigue or high dead spacePossible ventilatory failure or inadequate minute ventilation.
Metabolic acidosis/lactateMay reflect shock/sepsis and worsens overall oxygen delivery.

Chest X-ray

Chest imaging commonly shows bilateral opacities. Imaging alone is not enough; the RT should connect it to timing, oxygenation, PEEP/CPAP level, and whether edema is likely cardiogenic or noncardiogenic.

Severity & Red Flags

The key question is not simply “Does this patient have bilateral opacities?” The key question is whether oxygenation and lung mechanics are worsening despite appropriate support.

Red FlagWhy It Matters
PaO₂ remains low despite high FiO₂Suggests refractory hypoxemia and shunt physiology.
P/F ratio ≤100Severe ARDS category; escalation likely needed.
High plateau pressureIncreases risk of ventilator-induced lung injury.
Worsening complianceLungs are becoming stiffer and harder to ventilate safely.
Shock or rising lactateOxygen delivery is threatened even if SpO₂ looks acceptable.
Ventilator dyssynchronyCan worsen pressures, oxygenation, and patient work of breathing.

Management & Treatment

ARDS management is supportive and cause-directed. The core RT priorities are oxygenation support, lung-protective ventilation, appropriate PEEP, prevention of ventilator-induced lung injury, close monitoring, and escalation when hypoxemia remains severe.

Management Strategies

StrategyExampleWhy It Helps
Treat the causeAntibiotics/source control for sepsis or pneumoniaARDS will not resolve if the trigger continues.
Lung-protective ventilationLow VT based on predicted body weightReduces overdistention and ventilator-induced lung injury.
Pressure limitationPlateau pressure goal commonly <30 cm H₂OLimits alveolar stress and barotrauma risk.
PEEP optimizationPEEP/FiO₂ tables or individualized strategyRecruits alveoli and improves oxygenation while avoiding harm.
Prone positioningSevere ARDS with poor oxygenationImproves V/Q matching and recruits dependent lung regions.
Rescue escalationParalysis, inhaled pulmonary vasodilator, ECMO referral when ordered/appropriateUsed when conventional support fails.

Lung-Protective Ventilation Concept

The ventilator should oxygenate and ventilate while protecting the lung. Board-style ARDS questions often prioritize low tidal volume by predicted body weight, plateau pressure limitation, adequate PEEP, permissive hypercapnia when appropriate, and proning for severe hypoxemia.

ARDS Escalation & Ventilator Considerations

Severe ARDS may remain hypoxemic despite high FiO₂, PEEP, and lung-protective ventilation. These patients need early team communication, strict pressure monitoring, proning readiness, and rescue escalation planning.

Watch for:
P/F ≤100, refractory SpO₂/PaO₂, high plateau pressure, dyssynchrony, shock, worsening compliance.
Vent goal:
Support oxygenation while preventing volutrauma, barotrauma, atelectrauma, and oxygen toxicity.
Settings concept:
Use low VT, pressure limitation, adequate PEEP, and prone positioning when indicated.

Board Pearl

In ARDS requiring ventilation, avoid overdistention. Concepts usually include low tidal volume based on predicted body weight, plateau pressure monitoring, adequate PEEP, prone positioning for severe cases, and permissive hypercapnia when clinically appropriate and ordered.

Respiratory Therapist Role

The RT is central to ARDS assessment, ventilator management, oxygenation monitoring, proning support, patient safety, and escalation. The RT should trend SpO₂, ABGs, FiO₂, PEEP, P/F ratio, plateau pressure, driving pressure concepts, compliance, synchrony, secretions, positioning tolerance, and response to interventions.

FAQ

Common ARDS Questions

What is the simplest way to understand ARDS?

ARDS is diffuse inflammatory lung injury that makes the alveolar-capillary membrane leaky, floods/collapses alveoli, and causes severe hypoxemia.

How is ARDS different from cardiogenic pulmonary edema?

ARDS edema is primarily from increased permeability and inflammation, while cardiogenic pulmonary edema is primarily from hydrostatic pressure related to heart failure or fluid overload.

Why is the P/F ratio important?

The P/F ratio quantifies oxygenation impairment. In the Berlin framework, mild ARDS is 201–300, moderate is 101–200, and severe is 100 or less with PEEP/CPAP at least 5 cm H₂O.

Why use low tidal volume ventilation?

ARDS lungs are stiff and heterogenous. Low tidal volume based on predicted body weight helps reduce overdistention and ventilator-induced lung injury.

Why is PEEP used?

PEEP helps keep unstable alveoli open, improves recruitment, and supports oxygenation, but excessive PEEP can overdistend lung units and impair hemodynamics.

Why prone severe ARDS patients?

Prone positioning can improve V/Q matching, recruit dependent lung regions, and improve oxygenation in severe ARDS when performed safely by a trained team.

What does the RT monitor closely?

The RT trends SpO₂, ABGs, FiO₂, PEEP, P/F ratio, plateau pressure, compliance, synchrony, hemodynamics, airway security, and response to interventions.

Summary & Clinical Pearls

ARDS is an acute inflammatory lung injury that causes noncardiogenic pulmonary edema, alveolar collapse/flooding, shunt physiology, low compliance, and refractory hypoxemia. RT care centers on recognizing severity, supporting oxygenation, protecting the lung during ventilation, and escalating when conventional support is not enough.

Must know:
ARDS is acute hypoxemic respiratory failure with bilateral opacities not fully explained by heart failure/fluid overload.
Board pearl:
P/F ratio categories: mild 201–300, moderate 101–200, severe ≤100 with PEEP/CPAP ≥5.
RT pearl:
Trend FiO₂, PEEP, P/F ratio, plateau pressure, compliance, synchrony, and response to positioning/PEEP changes.
Common mistake:
Do not chase perfect CO₂ or SpO₂ with unsafe pressures or tidal volumes. Protect the lung.
