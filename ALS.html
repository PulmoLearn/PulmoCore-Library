Respiratory Disease Module

ALS

Amyotrophic lateral sclerosis is a progressive motor neuron disease that eventually weakens the diaphragm, intercostals, accessory muscles, bulbar muscles, and expiratory cough muscles. This module focuses on RT-level recognition of neuromuscular ventilatory failure, nocturnal hypoventilation, cough impairment, secretion clearance, NIV initiation, and escalation planning.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this ALS module, learners should connect motor neuron degeneration, respiratory muscle weakness, impaired cough, hypoventilation, diagnostic trends, NIV decisions, secretion management, and escalation planning.

1. Explain pathophysiology
Describe how motor neuron loss weakens inspiratory, expiratory, and bulbar muscles.
2. Interpret diagnostics
Use FVC/SVC, MIP/NIF, MEP, peak cough flow, ABG/VBG, and overnight oximetry/capnography.
3. Choose RT interventions
Match NIV, cough assist, suction, lung volume recruitment, and oxygen caution to the patient problem.
4. Escalate appropriately
Recognize acute infection, aspiration, secretion retention, hypercapnia, and inability to protect the airway.

Disease Snapshot

ALS does not begin as a lung parenchymal disease. The lungs may be structurally normal, but the pump fails because respiratory muscles progressively weaken. The RT must distinguish oxygenation problems from ventilation failure and avoid treating hypoventilation with oxygen alone.

What it is:
Progressive upper and lower motor neuron disease.
Primary respiratory problem:
Ventilatory pump failure from muscle weakness.
Classic symptoms:
Orthopnea, weak cough, morning headaches, fatigue, dyspnea, sleep disruption.
Key diagnostic clue:
Declining vital capacity and inspiratory/expiratory muscle pressures.
Acute danger:
Bulbar dysfunction, aspiration, retained secretions, hypercapnia, infection.
RT priority:
Assess ventilation, cough strength, secretion clearance, NIV fit/tolerance, and airway protection.

RT Clinical Connection

In ALS, normal SpO₂ can be misleading. A patient may be retaining CO₂ before oxygen saturation falls. RT assessment should include symptoms of hypoventilation, respiratory muscle metrics, cough effectiveness, and secretion clearance.

Board Prep Frame

Immediately associate ALS with progressive neuromuscular weakness, restrictive spirometry pattern, reduced vital capacity, weak cough, nocturnal hypoventilation, NIV support, cough assist, aspiration risk, and oxygen caution when ventilation is inadequate.

Anatomy & Pathophysiology

ALS damages motor neurons that control voluntary skeletal muscles. Respiratory decline occurs when inspiratory muscles cannot generate adequate tidal volume, expiratory muscles cannot cough effectively, and bulbar weakness impairs airway protection.

Motor neuron degeneration → diaphragm/intercostal weakness + weak expiratory muscles + bulbar dysfunction → nocturnal hypoventilation, ineffective cough, aspiration risk → hypercapnia, atelectasis, infection, ventilatory failure.

Danger Sign

New morning headaches, somnolence, confusion, or rising PaCO₂ in ALS suggests hypoventilation and should trigger urgent reassessment of ventilatory support.

Clinical Manifestations

ALS respiratory symptoms may be subtle at first. Students should think beyond dyspnea and look for sleep-related hypoventilation, weak cough, secretion difficulty, and aspiration clues.

Finding TypeWhat the RT May See
Sleep symptomsMorning headaches, daytime sleepiness, nightmares, frequent awakenings.
Breathing patternOrthopnea, shallow breathing, accessory muscle use, paradoxical breathing.
Cough/secretion cluesWeak cough, retained secretions, frequent suction need, recurrent infections.
Bulbar cluesDysarthria, dysphagia, choking, drooling, aspiration episodes.
Ventilation cluesRising PaCO₂, low tidal volume, reduced vital capacity, fatigue.
Oxygenation cluesDesaturation may occur late, during sleep, with infection, or with atelectasis.

Important Teaching Point

In neuromuscular disease, hypoxemia may be a late sign. A normal pulse oximeter reading does not rule out nocturnal hypoventilation or CO₂ retention.

Diagnostics & Assessment

ALS respiratory evaluation tracks muscle strength, ventilation, cough effectiveness, sleep-related hypoventilation, and complications such as atelectasis or pneumonia.

Respiratory Measures & Escalation Thresholds

ALS severity is often followed with trends rather than a single number. Declining vital capacity, worsening symptoms, poor cough, and CO₂ retention matter more than isolated SpO₂.

Early concern
Orthopnea, sleep symptoms, declining FVC/SVC, weak cough.
Moderate concern
Nocturnal hypoventilation, secretion retention, recurrent infections, low cough flow.
High concern
Daytime hypercapnia, aspiration, inability to clear secretions, altered mental status.

Severity & Red Flags

The key question is not simply “Is the saturation okay?” The key question is whether the patient can ventilate, protect the airway, and clear secretions.

Management & Treatment

ALS management is supportive and anticipatory. RT care focuses on ventilation support, airway clearance, secretion management, infection prevention, education, and planning before crisis occurs.

Ventilation Planning & Escalation

ALS respiratory failure is predictable, so escalation planning should happen before a crisis. NIV can improve symptoms and ventilation, but bulbar dysfunction, secretion burden, and patient goals shape the plan.

Watch for:
Hypercapnia, somnolence, aspiration, secretion retention, pneumonia, inability to tolerate NIV.
Vent goal:
Support alveolar ventilation, rest respiratory muscles, and improve sleep-related hypoventilation.
Planning concept:
Discuss NIV, cough assist, suction, advance directives, and tracheostomy preferences early.

Board Pearl

For ALS, oxygen is not the primary treatment for hypoventilation. If CO₂ is rising, the problem is ventilation. Think NIV and airway clearance, not oxygen alone.

Respiratory Therapist Role

The RT is central to monitoring respiratory muscle decline, supporting ventilation, optimizing NIV tolerance, strengthening airway clearance routines, educating caregivers, and recognizing when acute deterioration requires escalation.

FAQ

Common ALS Respiratory Questions

What is the simplest way to understand ALS respiratory failure?

The lungs may be normal, but the respiratory pump weakens. The patient cannot generate enough ventilation or cough force.

Why can SpO₂ look normal while the patient is failing?

Pulse oximetry measures oxygen saturation, not CO₂ clearance. ALS patients can retain CO₂ before oxygen saturation drops.

Why is oxygen alone risky?

Oxygen may improve saturation but does not correct hypoventilation. CO₂ can continue rising if ventilation is not supported.

What symptoms suggest nocturnal hypoventilation?

Morning headaches, daytime sleepiness, poor sleep, orthopnea, nightmares, and fatigue are common clues.

Why is cough assist important?

ALS weakens expiratory muscles. Cough assist helps clear secretions when the patient cannot generate enough cough flow.

How does bulbar dysfunction change management?

Bulbar weakness increases aspiration risk, secretion difficulty, and may make mask NIV less tolerable.

When should escalation planning occur?

Early. ALS decline is progressive, so NIV, airway clearance, emergency plans, and tracheostomy preferences should be discussed before crisis.

Summary & Clinical Pearls

ALS causes progressive neuromuscular respiratory failure. The key RT problem is not bronchospasm or primary alveolar damage; it is ventilatory pump weakness, impaired cough, secretion retention, aspiration risk, and eventual hypercapnia.

Must know:
ALS respiratory failure is primarily ventilatory muscle failure.
Board pearl:
Normal SpO₂ does not rule out hypoventilation or CO₂ retention.
RT pearl:
Trend FVC/SVC, MIP/NIF, MEP, cough flow, symptoms, and CO₂.
Common mistake:
Do not treat hypoventilation with oxygen alone; support ventilation and airway clearance.
